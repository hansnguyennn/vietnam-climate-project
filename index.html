The Project

Three Datasets. Four Questions. One Story.

Vietnam ranks among the world's top ten countries most affected by climate-related disasters. This project joins climate records, disaster histories, and river gauge data to ask: why? And is it getting worse?

Q1

Research Question 1

Does More Rain in a Month Mean More Disasters?

Average Monthly Rainfall · 1901–2020

Flood & Storm Events by Month

Deaths from Floods & Storms by Month

Key Finding

More rain does lead to more disasters — but with a delay. Rain peaks in August; disasters and deaths peak in September–October. The ground is already saturated when the season's worst storms arrive, leaving the water nowhere to go.

The three charts tell a sequential story. Rainfall builds through the summer monsoon, cresting around August. Flood and storm events follow roughly one month later, peaking in October. Deaths peak in September — earlier than events — because September combines still-high rainfall with early typhoon season, a particularly deadly window.

August has the heaviest average rainfall but does not produce the most deaths. That makes sense: by August the population has adapted to seasonal flooding. It's the surprise events in September and October, arriving on already-saturated ground, that kill the most people. This pattern aligns with research on compound flood risk in the Mekong and Red River basins.

Q2

Research Question 2

Do Provinces with Volatile Rivers Get Hit Harder by Disasters?

River Volatility — Level Range (cm)

Disaster Mentions per Province

Key Finding

Volatile rivers and frequent disasters do tend to cluster together — but central coast provinces like Hue and Quang Nam are disproportionately hit despite moderate river swings, because their narrow coastal geography means even average floods have nowhere to drain.

Song Da (Hoa Binh) and Song Ma (Thanh Hoa) show the largest water level ranges — their rivers can swing by thousands of centimeters across recorded history. Both provinces appear frequently in disaster records. That correlation is real.

But the central coast tells a different story. Hue (Song Ta Trach) and Quang Nam (Song Thu Bon) have more modest absolute river ranges, yet both provinces appear heavily in disaster records. The reason is geography: these provinces sit in narrow valleys wedged between the Truong Son mountains and the sea. When rain hits, it funnels directly through populated lowlands with little room to spread. Geography amplifies moderate volatility into severe outcomes.

Key Finding

Song Thu Bon and Song Ta Trach spend the most time above their danger thresholds — around 7% of all readings. That's roughly 25 dangerous days per year on average. Both are in central Vietnam, where typhoon-driven rainfall events push rivers past their tipping points repeatedly every season.

The danger threshold is defined as the mean water level plus two standard deviations — a level that should only be exceeded about 2–3% of the time under purely random variation. Rivers that exceed it far more often than that are structurally prone to dangerous flooding, not just occasionally unlucky.

Song Thu Bon and Song Ta Trach blow past that benchmark. Their central Vietnam location is the key: typhoons making landfall along Vietnam's narrow central coast bring intense, short-duration rainfall directly into their catchment areas. The rivers rise fast, cross the danger level, and the surrounding lowlands flood before drainage can occur. The Mekong tributary systems in the south show lower percentages partly because the floodplain is wide enough to absorb some of the surge.

Q4

Research Question 4

Has Vietnam's Rainfall Pattern Shifted Over 90 Years?

Average Monthly Precipitation — 1901–1930 vs 1991–2020

Month 1901–1930 (mm) 1991–2020 (mm) Change

Key Finding

November has gained approximately +11.6 mm of rainfall over 90 years. September has lost about -11.5 mm. The dangerous tail of the rainy season is getting longer — and the deadliest months (October–November, per Q1) are now wetter than they were a century ago.

The seasonal shape of Vietnam's rainfall hasn't inverted — the summer monsoon still dominates — but the edges have shifted. Early autumn (August–September) appears slightly drier than it was in the early 1900s, while late autumn (October–November) is measurably wetter. This is consistent with broader research on Southeast Asian monsoon intensification: rainfall becoming more concentrated and extreme at the margins of the wet season.

The policy implication is direct: since October and November already produce the most disasters and deaths (Q1), a wetter November extends that window. Communities that historically began demobilizing disaster preparedness after October are now seeing more late-season flood events. The data suggests this trend is not noise — it has been building steadily across four 30-year periods.
